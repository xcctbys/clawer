You know it's right when the companies who made money from other approaches - companies that usually can't agree on anything, mind you - get <a href="http://www.fiercecable.com/story/wireless-giants-get-behind-lte-u-laa-coalition/2015-09-28" rel="nofollow">together</a> to start trying to either own or subvert the new approach. That's what's going on in U.S. telecommunications right now.</p>
                            <p>Qualcomm (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/qcom' title='Qualcomm Inc.'>QCOM</a>), Verizon (NYSE:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/vz' title='Verizon Communications'>VZ</a>), AT&amp;T (NYSE:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/t' title='AT&T Inc.'>T</a>), T-Mobile (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/tmus' title='T-Mobile US, Inc.'>TMUS</a>), Alcatel-Lucent (NYSE:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/alu' title='Alcatel-Lucent'>ALU</a>) and others have gotten together to lobby the FCC on <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LTE_in_unlicensed_spectrum" rel="nofollow">LTE-U</a>, which stands for Long Term Evolution - Unlicensed. On the <a href="http://www.networkworld.com/article/2941873/wireless/lte-u-is-coming-to-take-your-wi-fi-away-consumer-advocates-warn.html" rel="nofollow">other side</a> of the argument, you have Google (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/goog' title='Alphabet Inc.'>GOOG</a>) (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/googl' title='Alphabet Inc.'>GOOGL</a>) and the WiFi alliance, which is sponsored by the likes of Apple (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/aapl' title='Apple Inc.'>AAPL</a>), Broadcom (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/brcm' title='Broadcom Corporation'>BRCM</a>), Cisco (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/csco' title='Cisco Systems, Inc.'>CSCO</a>), Dell (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/dell' title='Dell Inc.'>DELL</a>), Intel (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/intc' title='Intel Corporation'>INTC</a>), and Microsoft (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/msft' title='Microsoft Corporation'>MSFT</a>). Comcast (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/cmcsa' title='Comcast Corporation'>CMCSA</a>) (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/cmcsk' title='Comcast Corporation'>CMCSK</a>) is also a WiFi alliance sponsor, and virtually the entire rest of the cable industry is brought into the fray with support from the CableLabs consortium. This article previews Qualcomm earnings in light of the company's long-term outlook, which I believe is dominated by the changing wireless landscape.</p>
                            <h2 class="title">Earnings &amp; Market Dynamics</h2>
                            <p><img src="https://staticseekingalpha.a.ssl.fastly.net/uploads/2015/10/26/767775-14458891337607937-Esekla.png" align="left" alt="QCOM 5 yrchart" hspace="6" vspace="6" width="284" height="150" />Qualcomm has scheduled its fourth quarter earnings call after the market close next Wednesday. On average analysts expect the company to earn 86 cents per share on revenue of $5.2 billion. First quarter revenue expectations average out to $5.8 billion. These results would be substantially below any quarter in recent memory, and while QCOM has taken a 20% dive over the past year, it has rebounded by ~15% in the past month to a level more consistent with its 5-year valuation.</p>

                            <p>The bigger picture is even more concerning. Although the put to call ratio for options near current value is 0.67, the total put to call ratio is 0.88. Furthermore, there are substantial puts all the way down to the $40 strike, which has an Open Interest of 2,244 contracts, whereas calls only extend $10 above current prices with 1,304 contracts open at 70. While one might also argue that the market is already pricing in disappointment, and that Qualcomm needs to merely hold the line to avoid a big price drop, my opinion is that short term pricing like this is a game that individual investors <a href="http://seekingalpha.com/article/1802882-the-next-momo-stock-part-1-understanding-the-momentum-mechanism">simply cannot win</a> as a group. I note that call buyers seldom turn out to be the smart money, and think this distribution is not positive for the stockholders going into earnings.</p>
                            <h2 class="title">Long Term (Evolution Uncertainty)</h2>
                            <p>I prefer to remain focused on the long term picture, but here again, the future is uncertain at best. Qualcomm has made its fortune mainly by owning the protocols for wireless consumer wireless communications and selling chipsets that leverage those protocols. According to Trefis, Mobile Device Royalties comprise over 53% of the company's value; the rest is about evenly split between chip sales and net cash.</p>
                            <p>As I highlighted in the introduction, the main portion of Qualcomm's revenue is being called into question as the industry contemplates the next generation of LTE. Qualcomm is attempting to <a href="http://apps.fcc.gov/ecfs/document/view?id=60001121996" rel="nofollow">unify</a> competitors under the LTE-U standard and <a href="http://www.fiercewireless.com/story/qualcomms-new-snapdragon-820-chipset-will-enable-lte-u-phones-starting-2016/2015-09-15" rel="nofollow">push</a> out new chipsets. However, it is doing so in the face of industry and regulatory <a href="https://www.publicknowledge.org/press-release/senators-call-on-fcc-to-examine-lte-u-technology" rel="nofollow">scrutiny</a>. Furthermore, unlike in the past, Qualcomm is making this attempt at time when there is already a completely viable alternative already in place. Rather than continuing to license technology from Qualcomm and buy the associated chipsets, vendors now have the option of simply using WiFi, which carries virtually no licensing fees.</p>
                            <p>While there is certainly room to <a href="http://www.fiercewireless.com/tech/story/study-poor-management-wi-fi-assets-could-cost-operators-18b-lost-revenue/2015-09-27" rel="nofollow">improve</a> WiFi particularly when it comes to multiple band use and congestion resolution, the history of technology shows that progress is best achieved through the formation of standards that are not owned by any single corporation. WiFi itself is perhaps the best example of this, as it runs entirely in spectrum that was once thought to be completely unusable. Ultimately, all walled gardens <a href="http://seekingalpha.com/article/2553475-3-signs-that-apples-next-breakout-may-be-its-last">eventually decay</a> while open technologies tend to show much longer and more flexible lifetimes. The future of LTE-U is far from certain, and I believe its success, or lack thereof, will be the largest single factor affecting Qualcomm's long term future.</p>
                            <h2 class="title">Other Factors and Outside Implications</h2>
                            <p>What's more, there is little that Qualcomm or any other company can do about this shift. As a result, I think the smart institutional money is looking to cash out. We see <a href="http://seekingalpha.com/article/2553475-3-signs-that-apples-next-breakout-may-be-its-last#comment-41565095">efforts like this</a> all the time, as companies transition growth to value trap, but the difference here is that management seems to be <a href="https://www.qualcomm.com/news/releases/2015/07/22/qualcomm-announces-strategic-realignment-plan" rel="nofollow">giving in</a>, even though management knows it <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2015/09/03/us-qualcomm-president-idUSKCN0R32HL20150903" rel="nofollow">may not be</a> in the best interest of long-term common shareholders. In this case, Jana Partners pulled a very similar stunt with Outerwall (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/outr' title='Outerwall Inc.'>OUTR</a>). While that did result in a <a href="http://seekingalpha.com/article/1516722-the-next-short-squeeze#comment-24152102">temporary bump</a> in share price, I think it's now pretty much beyond argument that the measures instigated by Jana were a horrible use of shareholder equity. If Jana was so interested in shareholder value, why didn't they buy the shares at $70 each in the dutch auction that they pushed on management, instead of dumping them?</p>
                            <p>Looking beyond Qualcomm, I've <a href="http://seekingalpha.com/article/3395395-googles-project-fi-who-gets-hurt">already written</a> about how the growth of unlicensed spectrum could negatively affect Dish Network (NASDAQ:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/dish' title='DISH Network Corporation'>DISH</a>), and the company is already <a href="http://seekingalpha.com/article/3395395-googles-project-fi-who-gets-hurt#comment-61191306">starting to suffer</a> as predicted. By contrast, while Google certainly prefers the WiFi option, it is in a position to be flexible on how things develop with its already proven <a href="http://seekingalpha.com/article/3377675-a-first-hand-look-at-the-google-fi-opportunity#comment-57563325">network of networks</a>. However, although Google has turned in a good earnings report, I think investors are seriously underestimating the potential for government interference and penalties. Qualcomm has largely put such issues behind it, at least for the time being. I think such wrangling, particularly in China, is the primary reason for this year's drop in QCOM shares; Google investors would do well to look at just how big an impact such issues had on Qualcomm shares.</p>
                            <p>There's something for almost everyone else in this shift, though. While Verizon and AT&amp;T, as top dogs of the wireless industry, will give at least token support to LTE-U, as it helps maintain the status quo. That said, even they have to see benefit in no longer needed to pay expensive licensing fees for spectrum. AT&amp;T has already gone ahead and gotten a <a href="http://appleinsider.com/articles/15/10/06/att-granted-fcc-waiver-to-activate-wi-fi-calling-amid-tiff-with-t-mobile-sprint" rel="nofollow">waiver</a> to allow it to route calls over WiFi. The increasing use of unlicensed spectrum probably also has something to do with Sprint (NYSE:<a href='http://seekingalpha.com/symbol/s' title='Sprint Corporation'>S</a>) <a href="http://www.lightreading.com/mobile/spectrum/sprint-says-it-will-sit-out-incentive-auction-/d/d-id/718438" rel="nofollow">sitting out</a> the next spectrum auction entirely. Cable companies would absolutely love to be able to leverage their existing wired networks for wireless backhaul, which is why they are trying merge to increase scale, and for chip makers like Intel and Broadcom, this is a new angle to help them squeeze into the coveted mobile market. Last, and probably least in the minds of these big players, I think it's clear that a shift to open technology and unlicensed spectrum would bring much needed competition to the industry and that consumers would benefit enormously from such competition rather than a continuation of the current duopoly.</p>
                            <h2 class="title">Valuation &amp; Conclusion</h2>
                            <p>Major shifts in consumer wireless threaten Qualcomm's historical revenue, and the evidence suggests to me that future sales issues are not yet priced in. Nonetheless, with a trailing PE of 17, Qualcomm's moderate valuation, substantial <a href="http://seekingalpha.com/article/2545185-invensense-gt-advanced-and-the-importance-of-the-balance-sheet">balance sheet</a> with Quick and Current ratios above 4, 3%+ dividend, and likely volatility due to institutional meddling disqualify it for consideration as an immediate short. Rather, I see Qualcomm as being at the early stages of transitioning to becoming dead money, much like Microsoft became for a decade or more after its Windows franchise had matured at the turn of the century. All good things come to end; fortunately for consumers, so do bad ones.</p>
